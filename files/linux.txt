</p><p>He was honored, along with <a href="/wiki/Shinya_Yamanaka" title="Shinya Yamanaka">Shinya Yamanaka</a>, with the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Technology_Prize" title="Millennium Technology Prize">Millennium Technology Prize</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Technology_Academy_Finland" title="Technology Academy Finland">Technology Academy Finland</a> "in recognition of his creation of a new open source operating system for computers leading to the widely used Linux kernel."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He is also the recipient of the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Computer_Pioneer_Award" title="Computer Pioneer Award">IEEE Computer Society Computer Pioneer Award</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the 2018 <a href="/wiki/IEEE_Masaru_Ibuka_Consumer_Electronics_Award" title="IEEE Masaru Ibuka Consumer Electronics Award">IEEE Masaru Ibuka Consumer Electronics Award</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ibuka_rl_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibuka_rl-6">[6]</a></sup>

<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#The_Linus/Linux_connection"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">The Linus/Linux connection</span></a></li>
<p>Torvalds was born in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, Finland, the son of journalists Anna and <a href="/wiki/Nils_Torvalds" title="Nils Torvalds">Nils Torvalds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the grandson of statistician <a href="/wiki/Leo_T%C3%B6rnqvist" title="Leo Törnqvist">Leo Törnqvist</a> and of poet <a href="/wiki/Ole_Torvalds" title="Ole Torvalds">Ole Torvalds</a>, and the great-grandson of journalist and soldier <a href="/wiki/Toivo_Karanko" title="Toivo Karanko">Toivo Karanko</a>. His parents were campus radicals at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a> in the 1960s. His family belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Swedish-speaking_Finns" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish-speaking Finns">Swedish-speaking minority</a> in Finland. He was named after <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a>, the Nobel Prize-winning American chemist, although in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Code" title="Rebel Code">Rebel Code</a>: Linux and the Open Source Revolution</i>, he is quoted as saying, "I think I was named equally for <a href="/wiki/Linus_van_Pelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Linus van Pelt">Linus</a> the <a href="/wiki/Peanuts" title="Peanuts">Peanuts</a> cartoon character", noting that this made him "half Nobel Prize-winning chemist and half blanket-carrying cartoon character".<sup id="cite_ref-moody_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moody-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torvalds attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a> from 1988 to 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> graduating with a master's degree in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> from the NODES research group.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His academic career was interrupted after his first year of study when he joined the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Navy" title="Finnish Navy">Finnish Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Nyland_Brigade" title="Nyland Brigade">Nyland Brigade</a> in the summer of 1989, selecting the 11-month officer training program to fulfill the <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Finland" title="Conscription in Finland">mandatory military service of Finland</a>. He gained the rank of <a href="/wiki/Second_lieutenant" title="Second lieutenant">second lieutenant</a>, with the role of an <a href="/wiki/Artillery_observer" title="Artillery observer">artillery observer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He bought computer science professor <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Tanenbaum" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew Tanenbaum">Andrew Tanenbaum</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Operating_Systems:_Design_and_Implementation" title="Operating Systems: Design and Implementation">Operating Systems: Design and Implementation</a></i>, in which Tanenbaum describes <a href="/wiki/MINIX" class="mw-redirect" title="MINIX">MINIX</a>, an educational stripped-down version of <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>. In 1990, Torvalds resumed his university studies, and was exposed to Unix for the first time in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a> <a href="/wiki/MicroVAX" title="MicroVAX">MicroVAX</a> running <a href="/wiki/ULTRIX" class="mw-redirect" title="ULTRIX">ULTRIX</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His MSc thesis was titled <i><a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>: A Portable Operating System</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>His interest in computers began with a <a href="/wiki/Commodore_VIC-20" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodore VIC-20">Commodore VIC-20</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> at the age of 11 in 1981. He started programming for it in <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a>, then later by directly accessing the <a href="/wiki/MOS_Technology_6502" title="MOS Technology 6502">6502</a> CPU in <a href="/wiki/Machine_code" title="Machine code">machine code</a> (he did not utilize <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly language</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He then purchased a <a href="/wiki/Sinclair_QL" title="Sinclair QL">Sinclair QL</a>, which he modified extensively, especially its operating system. "Because it was so hard to get software for it in Finland", he wrote his own assembler and <a href="/wiki/Text_editor" title="Text editor">editor</a> "(in addition to <a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a> graphics libraries)"<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> for the QL, and a few games.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He wrote a <i><a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a></i> clone, <i>Cool Man</i>. On 5 January 1991<sup id="cite_ref-first_interview_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-first_interview-19">[19]</a></sup> he purchased an <a href="/wiki/Intel_80386" class="mw-redirect" title="Intel 80386">Intel 80386</a>-based clone of <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">IBM PC</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> before receiving his <a href="/wiki/MINIX" class="mw-redirect" title="MINIX">MINIX</a> copy, which in turn enabled him to begin work on Linux.
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linux">Linux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linus_Torvalds&action=edit&section=3" title="Edit section: Linux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Linux" title="History of Linux">History of Linux</a></div>
<p>The first Linux prototypes were publicly released in late 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-moody_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moody-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Version 1.0 was released on 14 March 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-kern1p0-release_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kern1p0-release-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torvalds first encountered the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Project" title="GNU Project">GNU Project</a> in 1991 when another Swedish-speaking computer science student, Lars Wirzenius, took him to the University of Technology to listen to free software guru <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stallman" title="Richard Stallman">Richard Stallman</a>'s speech.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Torvalds used Stallman's <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a> version 2 (GPLv2) for his <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a>.
</p><p>After a visit to <a href="/wiki/Transmeta" title="Transmeta">Transmeta</a> in late 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-linuxonlinebio_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linuxonlinebio-23">[23]</a></sup> Torvalds accepted a position at the company in California, where he worked from February 1997 to June 2003. He then moved to the <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_Development_Labs" title="Open Source Development Labs">Open Source Development Labs</a>, which has since merged with the <a href="/wiki/Free_Standards_Group" title="Free Standards Group">Free Standards Group</a> to become the <a href="/wiki/Linux_Foundation" title="Linux Foundation">Linux Foundation</a>, under whose auspices he continues to work. In June 2004, Torvalds and his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Dunthorpe,_Oregon" title="Dunthorpe, Oregon">Dunthorpe, Oregon</a><sup id="cite_ref-Rogoway_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogoway-24">[24]</a></sup> to be closer to the OSDL's headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Beaverton,_Oregon" title="Beaverton, Oregon">Beaverton</a>.
</p><p>From 1997 to 1999, he was involved in <a href="/wiki/86open" class="mw-redirect" title="86open">86open</a>, helping select the standard binary format for <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>. In 1999, he was named by the <i><a href="/wiki/MIT_Technology_Review" title="MIT Technology Review">MIT Technology Review</a></i> <a href="/wiki/TR35" class="mw-redirect" title="TR35">TR100</a> as one of the world's top 100 innovators under age 35.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Red_Hat" title="Red Hat">Red Hat</a> and <a href="/wiki/VA_Linux" class="mw-redirect" title="VA Linux">VA Linux</a>, both leading developers of Linux-based software, presented Torvalds with <a href="/wiki/Stock_option" class="mw-redirect" title="Stock option">stock options</a> in gratitude for his creation.<sup id="cite_ref-r1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-26">[26]</a></sup> That year both companies <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">went public</a> and Torvalds's share value briefly shot up to about US$20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unauthbio_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unauthbio-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>His personal mascot is a penguin nicknamed <a href="/wiki/Tux_(mascot)" title="Tux (mascot)">Tux</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which has been widely adopted by the Linux community as the Linux kernel's mascot.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Torvalds believes "<a href="/wiki/Open_source_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source model">open source</a> is the only right way to do software", he also has said that he uses the "best tool for the job", even if that includes <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary software</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He was criticized for his use and alleged advocacy of the proprietary <a href="/wiki/BitKeeper" title="BitKeeper">BitKeeper</a> software for version control in the Linux kernel. He subsequently wrote a free-software replacement for it called <a href="/wiki/Git" title="Git">Git</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, Torvalds stated that he used the <a href="/wiki/Fedora_Linux" title="Fedora Linux">Fedora Linux</a> distribution because it had fairly good support for the <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> processor architecture, which he favored at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-LinusFedora_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LinusFedora-32">[32]</a></sup> He confirmed this in a 2012 interview.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He has also posted updates about his choice of <a href="/wiki/Desktop_environment" title="Desktop environment">desktop environment</a>, often in response to <a href="/wiki/GNOME#Criticism" title="GNOME">perceived feature regressions</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Linux_Foundation" title="Linux Foundation">Linux Foundation</a> currently sponsors Torvalds so he can work full-time on improving Linux.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torvalds is known for vocally disagreeing with other developers on the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel_mailing_list" title="Linux kernel mailing list">Linux kernel mailing list</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Calling himself a "really unpleasant person", he explained, "I'd like to be a nice person and curse less and encourage people to grow rather than telling them they are idiots. I'm sorry—I tried, it's just not in me."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> His attitude, which he considers necessary for making his points clear, has drawn criticism from <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> programmer <a href="/wiki/Sage_Sharp" title="Sage Sharp">Sage Sharp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systemd" title="Systemd">systemd</a> developer <a href="/wiki/Lennart_Poettering" title="Lennart Poettering">Lennart Poettering</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2022)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>On Sunday, 16 September 2018, the Linux kernel <i>Code of Conflict</i> was suddenly replaced by a new <i>Code of Conduct</i> based on the <a href="/wiki/Contributor_Covenant" title="Contributor Covenant">Contributor Covenant</a>. Shortly thereafter, in the release notes for Linux 4.19-rc4, Torvalds apologized for his behavior, calling his personal attacks of the past "unprofessional and uncalled for" and announced a period of "time off" to "get some assistance on how to understand people's emotions and respond appropriately". It soon transpired that these events followed <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> approaching Torvalds with a series of questions critical of his conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Following the release of Linux 4.19 on 22 October 2018, Torvalds returned to maintaining the kernel.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
<h2><span id="The_Linus.2FLinux_connection"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Linus/Linux_connection">The Linus/Linux connection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linus_Torvalds&action=edit&section=4" title="Edit section: The Linus/Linux connection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Linux#Naming" title="History of Linux">History of Linux § Naming</a></div>
<p>Initially, Torvalds wanted to call the kernel he developed <i>Freax</i> (a combination of "free", "freak", and the letter X to indicate that it was a Unix-like system), but his friend <a href="/wiki/Ari_Lemmke" class="mw-redirect" title="Ari Lemmke">Ari Lemmke</a>, who administered the <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">FTP</a> <a href="/wiki/Server_(computing)" title="Server (computing)">server</a> where the kernel was first hosted, named Torvalds' directory <i>linux</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
<p>As of 2006, approximately 2% of the Linux kernel was written by Torvalds himself.<sup id="cite_ref-unauthbio_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unauthbio-28">[28]</a></sup> Because thousands have contributed to it, his percentage is still one of the largest. However, he said in 2012 that his own personal contribution is now mostly merging code written by others, with little programming.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> He retains the highest authority to decide which new code is incorporated into the standard Linux kernel.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torvalds holds the <i>Linux</i> trademark<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and monitors its use,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> chiefly through the <a href="/wiki/Linux_Mark_Institute" title="Linux Mark Institute">Linux Mark Institute</a>.
<p>Torvalds is married to Tove Torvalds (née Monni), a six-time Finnish national <a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">karate</a> champion, whom he met in late 1993. He was running introductory computer laboratory exercises for students and instructed the course attendees to send him an e-mail as a test, to which Tove responded with an e-mail asking for a date.<sup id="cite_ref-moody_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moody-8">[8]</a></sup> They were later married and have three daughters, two of whom were born in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-oreg100914_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oreg100914-51">[51]</a></sup> The Linux kernel's <i>reboot</i> system call accepts their dates of birth (written in hexadecimal) as <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(programming)" title="Magic number (programming)">magic values</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reboot2_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reboot2-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torvalds has described himself as "completely <a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">a-religious</a>—<a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>", adding, "I find that people seem to think religion brings morals and appreciation of nature. I actually think it detracts from both. It gives people the excuse to say, 'Oh, nature was just created,' and so the act of creation is seen to be something miraculous. I appreciate the fact that, 'Wow, it's incredible that something like this could have happened in the first place.<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>" He later added that while in Europe religion is mostly a personal issue, in the United States it has become very politicized. When discussing the issue of <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">church and state separation</a>, he said, "Yeah, it's kind of ironic that in many European countries, there is actually a kind of legal binding between the state and the <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">state religion</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In "Linus the Liberator", a story about the March <a href="/wiki/LinuxWorld_Conference_and_Expo" title="LinuxWorld Conference and Expo">LinuxWorld</a> Conference, Torvalds says: "There are like two golden rules in life. One is 'Do unto others as you would want them to do unto you.' For some reason, people associate this with Christianity. I'm not a Christian. I'm agnostic. The other rule is 'Be proud of what you do.<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
<td><a href="/wiki/IEEE_Masaru_Ibuka_Consumer_Electronics_Award" title="IEEE Masaru Ibuka Consumer Electronics Award">IEEE Masaru Ibuka Consumer Electronics Award</a> is conferred by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> for outstanding contributions to consumer electronics technology has been named in honor of the co-founder and honorary chairman of Sony Corporation, Masaru Ibuka. 2018 Ibuka award was conferred to Linus Torvalds "For his leadership of the development and proliferation of Linux."<sup id="cite_ref-ibuka_rl_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibuka_rl-6">[6]</a></sup>
<td>In 2008, he was inducted into the Hall of Fellows of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">Mountain View, California</a>, "for the creation of the Linux kernel and the management of open source development of the widely used Linux operating system."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
<td>In 2003, the naming of the asteroid moon <a href="/wiki/Linus_(moon)" title="Linus (moon)">Linus</a> was motivated in part by the fact that the discoverer was an enthusiastic Linux user. Although the naming proposal referred to the mythological <a href="/wiki/Linus_(mythology)" title="Linus (mythology)">Linus</a>, son of the muse <a href="/wiki/Calliope" title="Calliope">Calliope</a> and the inventor of melody and rhythm, the name was also meant to honor Linus Torvalds, and <a href="/wiki/Linus_van_Pelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Linus van Pelt">Linus van Pelt</a>, a character in the <a href="/wiki/Peanuts" title="Peanuts">Peanuts</a> comic strip.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
<p><i>InfoWorld</i> presented him with the 2000 Award for Industry Achievement.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In 2005, Torvalds appeared as one of "the best managers" in a survey by <i><a href="/wiki/BusinessWeek" class="mw-redirect" title="BusinessWeek">BusinessWeek</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Business_2.0" title="Business 2.0">Business 2.0</a></i> magazine named him one of "10 people who don't matter" because the growth of Linux has shrunk Torvalds's individual impact.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 11 October 2017, the Linux company <a href="/wiki/SUSE" title="SUSE">SUSE</a> made a song titled "Linus Said".<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
